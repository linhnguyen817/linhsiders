As the final project for 6.08: Embedded Systems at MIT, we were tasked to develop a creative and complete Internet of Things system.

Essentially, we were told to do something interesting and hard.

With three other teammates, four weeks, and a budget of $80, I helped build Superdrobe, a smart wardrobe mirror that helps make mornings and laundry days easier for busy people like myself and so many other students at MIT.

Superdrobe is a mirror-based smart device that:

keeps inventory of all items in user's wardrobe

suggests outfits based on occasion, weather, and user preferences

tracks number of dirty clothes and notifies user when to do laundry

informs user of currently available laundry machines in their building

Team: Will Archer, John Lin, Gary Nguyen, Linh Nguyen

Skills: Internet of Things, Project Management, Teamwork, UX Design
Programming: C++, Python, SQL, HTML, CSS, Javascript

A Personalized Experience

Wardrobe tracker website: "Yes, I have 10 pairs of black leggings, and yes, they are all different."

We use a clean, simple web interface for user input on wardrobe items and settings because of the limitations of the ESP32 screen and sensors for inputting large amounts of text. 

Users can add, delete, and update items from their wardrobe and input profile information through a friendly website UI


With user-defined settings, Superdrobe suggests weather-appropriate outfits and displays current laundry machine availability

Smart outfit suggestions: For those mornings when you have "NOTHING" to wear

From user research, we discovered that people have varying preferences in how involved they wanted to be in picking out individual parts of their outfits. To address these needs, Superdrobe features two different options to help users select outfits.

Make me an outfit presents combinations of wardrobe pieces to make a complete outfit for users who don’t want to go step-by-step selecting each piece of their outfit.

Help me make an outfit filters and sorts wardrobe pieces based on the occasion, preference, and weather for users who want the autonomy to select each piece themselves.

Laundry assistance: When you consider buying new socks instead of doing the laundry

When all items of a particular type (tops, bottoms, and/or outerwear) have run out of clean wears, Superdrobe notifies the user that a laundry day is in order. Laundry is tracked by comparing the max number of wears of each item set by users and its current number of wears based off of previously selected suggested outfits.

For busy students, information about washing and dryer machine availability for their building is provided in the “Check Laundry” screen. This information is collected through user input on their dorm building and webscraping the Laundry View site.

Behind the Mirror

Our DIY mirror

The mirror prototype is built using a shadow box (that also houses our wires and breadboard very nicely), mirrored window film, and ESP32 LCD display.

For user input, we implemented a scrolling mechanism using three copper sensors on the side of the mirror. There is audio feedback so users know if their taps on the buttons were detected.

Takeaways

"Our code won't matter if no one wants to use the product!"

From my previous experiences of developing customer-facing products at Microsoft, I wanted to prioritize the UX design for Superdrobe from the get-go because of how easy it is to get lost in the engineering aspects, especially when all of us are majoring in CS.

I encouraged all my teammates to get involved with the user research and design work early on and throughout the project with 1:1's and team brainstorming sessions. This helped us always keep the user in mind as we were building Superdrobe and overcoming many of our engineering challenges. -INSERT FUN GIFS HERE-

4 engineers on the loose!

Our instructors designed this project to be very open-ended with little guidelines on design and implementation.

With 4 team members, there were many different ideas and opinions on how things should be done, in addition to many dependencies from the hardware to the client-server side that needed to be pieced together. I took initiative to practice my project management skills by introducing and maintaining weekly SCRUM boards with tasks and milestones for each team member.

All of us had different personalities and skills, so encouraging everyone to voice their individual perspectives and ideas was also a big priority for me during our team meetings.

These efforts in addition to the great contributions from my teammates facilitated a team environment that made bringing the many pieces of the project together a seamless process in which everyone was proud of not only the final product, but also the journey getting there.

What's next?!

After 6.08 ended, the plan was for me to keep our Superdrobe prototype in my dorm room and keep tweaking and improving it throughout the year.

However, over the summer, our instructors reset the servers that Superdrobe relied on for its databases and HTTP requests to track the user's wardrobe items, an essential component to Superdrobe's many features.

At the very least, I now have a nice mirror to hang in my dorm room.
